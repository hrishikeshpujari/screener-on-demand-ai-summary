📊 Understanding the Scoring System

Every stock is scored out of 32 points across four frameworks — 8 points each. Each point is binary: pass or fail. No partial credit. This makes scores directly comparable across stocks and time.

🏦 Graham /8
Safety & value — low P/E, low P/B, strong balance sheet, dividend, growth history
🎩 Buffett /8
Quality & moat — high ROE, FCF, gross margin, ROIC, management quality
🔍 Lynch /8
Growth & story — low PEG, EPS growth, reasonable P/E, sales growth
📈 Technical /8
Price action — trend, EMA positions, breakouts, pullbacks (Al Brooks method)
Key insight: A stock can score high fundamentally but have poor technicals — great business, bad timing. Wait for the technical score to improve before entering. Conversely, strong technicals with weak fundamentals = momentum trade, not a value investment.
🚦 Reading BUY / HOLD / SELL Signals
✅ BUY — 22+ / 32
Strong across multiple frameworks. Best when both Fund /24 AND Tech /8 are strong. A BUY with poor technicals = wait for a better entry point.
⏳ HOLD — 13–21 / 32
Mixed signals. If you own it, hold. Don't add. If you don't own it, wait for fundamentals or technicals to improve before initiating a position.
🚫 SELL — 0–12 / 32
Fails most criteria. Review your thesis if you own it. Don't initiate new positions. Re-analyze in 1–2 quarters to check for improvement.
Confidence levels: ★★★ High (26+) = strong conviction across all frameworks. ★★ Medium (20–25) = good but mixed signals. ★ Low / Avoid (<20) = weak evidence, treat the signal skeptically.
📚 Fundamentals — what each number actually means

Reference for every metric the screener pulls. Each card shows the field name (so you can cross-check Finnhub directly), the formula, the threshold the screener uses, and a pitfall — when the number lies or needs context.

📊 Valuation Multiples
P/E Ratio  Finnhub peTTM
Price ÷ trailing 12-month EPS. What the market pays per dollar of earnings. Threshold: <15 green · <25 yellow · ≥25 red. Pitfall: Finnhub's peTTM is slightly smoothed (some one-time items excluded), so it can disagree with TradingView/Yahoo's strict GAAP P/E by 2-3x on stocks with recent one-time charges. For transition-year stocks, cross-check forward P/E on StockAnalysis.com.
P/B Ratio  Finnhub pb
Market cap ÷ book value of equity. How much you pay per dollar of net assets on the balance sheet. Threshold: <1.5 (Graham value) · <3 (acceptable) · >5 (premium). Pitfall: less useful for asset-light businesses — software companies routinely trade at 10×+ P/B because book value doesn't capture brand or IP value.
PEG Ratio  Finnhub pegTTM
P/E ÷ expected EPS growth rate. A 30× P/E company growing 30% has PEG = 1.0 — same value-per-growth as a 10× P/E growing 10%. Threshold: <1 (Lynch bargain) · 1–2 (reasonable) · >2 (expensive). Pitfall: depends entirely on analyst growth estimate. For low-coverage stocks, comes back null. Garbage in, garbage out on speculative growth assumptions.
💪 Profitability
Gross Margin  Finnhub grossMarginTTM
(Revenue − Cost of Goods Sold) ÷ Revenue. The pricing power signal — high gross margin means the company can charge well above its production cost. Threshold: ≥40% (Buffett moat) · 25–40% (decent) · <25% (commodity-like). Software businesses hit 70%+, retailers struggle to maintain 30%.
Net Margin  Finnhub netProfitMarginTTM
Net Income ÷ Revenue. Final profit per dollar of sales after everything — costs, interest, taxes, the works. Threshold: >5% (Graham minimum) · >10% (healthy) · >20% (exceptional). Pitfall: sensitive to one-time items same as P/E. A big writedown or restructuring crushes net margin temporarily without changing the underlying business.
ROE — Return on Equity  Finnhub roeTTM
Net Income ÷ Shareholders' Equity. How efficiently the business turns shareholder money into profit. Buffett's favorite single metric. Threshold: ≥15% (good) · ≥20% (excellent). Pitfall: can be inflated by heavy debt — debt reduces the equity denominator and lifts ROE without any improvement in business quality. Always check alongside debt/equity.
ROIC — Return on Invested Capital  computed
Operating profit ÷ (Debt + Equity). Better than ROE for debt-heavy companies because it accounts for all the capital, not just shareholders' slice. Threshold: ≥12% (excellent). When ROIC and ROE are both high and close to each other, the moat is real and debt-free.
📈 Growth
Revenue Growth  Finnhub revenueGrowth5Y · revenueGrowthTTMYoy
Compound annual growth in sales over 5 years, and latest 12 months vs prior 12 months. Top-line growth is harder to fake than earnings — revenue recognition has some accounting wiggle room, but bottom-line manipulation has much more. Threshold: >10% YoY (Lynch fast grower) · >0 (Graham minimum just for direction).
EPS Growth  Finnhub epsGrowth5Y · epsGrowthTTMYoy
Earnings per share growth over 5 years (CAGR) and year-over-year. The 5-year smooths cyclicality; YoY captures momentum. Threshold: >10% YoY (Lynch). Pitfall: EPS growth can be juiced by buybacks — fewer shares mean higher EPS without any business improvement. Cross-check with revenue growth: if EPS grows 15% but revenue is flat, it's financial engineering, not business growth.
🏦 Financial Health
Current Ratio  Finnhub currentRatioQuarterly
Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities. Can the company pay its near-term bills without raising emergency cash? Threshold: ≥2 (Graham conservative) · ≥1.5 (acceptable) · <1 (red flag — short-term obligations exceed liquid assets).
Debt / Equity  Finnhub totalDebt/totalEquityQuarterly
Total debt ÷ shareholders' equity. How leveraged is the balance sheet. Threshold: ≤0.5 (Graham low debt) · ≤1.0 (manageable) · >2 (highly leveraged). Pitfall: financials and utilities normally run higher — context by sector matters. A bank at 5× is normal; a software company at 5× is alarming.
Long-Term Debt / Equity  Finnhub longTermDebt/equityQuarterly
Strips out short-term operating debt to focus on structural leverage — bonds, term loans, long-dated obligations. Lynch wanted <30% — companies that don't depend on debt to fuel growth. Threshold: <30% (Lynch).
Free Cash Flow (FCF)  Finnhub series
Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditures. The actual cash left over after the business reinvests in itself. Buffett's reality check: "Earnings without FCF are accounting fiction." Positive and growing FCF underpins dividends, buybacks, and debt paydown. Negative FCF in a growth company isn't always bad — it can mean heavy capex for future expansion. Negative FCF in a mature company is a red flag.
💰 Cash Returns to Shareholders
Dividend Yield  Finnhub currentDividendYieldTTM
Annual dividend per share ÷ current share price. Returns 0 for companies that don't pay dividends. Pitfall: a very high yield (>6%) is often a "yield trap" — the market expects the dividend to be cut and is pricing the stock low in anticipation. Cross-check with payout ratio (dividend ÷ EPS) — anything >80% is fragile.
Cash per Share  Finnhub cashPerSharePerShareQuarterly
Net cash on the balance sheet ÷ shares outstanding. Lynch liked companies with cash reserves — they can survive downturns and fund growth without diluting shareholders. A company trading near its cash-per-share value has a floor.
🎯 Quality & Conviction Signals
ROE Consistency (years)  computed from series.annual.roe
Consecutive years ROE has been ≥15%. Streaks of 7+ years signal a structural moat — a one-year ROE spike could be luck, but a decade of high ROE is real competitive advantage. The single best historical predictor of future ROE.
Insider Ownership  ⚠ unavailable on this build
Percent of shares held by management. Lynch's "skin in the game" check (≥5%). Honest status: the Finnhub free tier doesn't expose this field, so it's hardcoded to 0 in the screener. The Lynch framework originally penalized every stock for failing this check (capping Lynch at 7/8); the criterion has been replaced with "Reasonable P/E (< 25)" — a GARP check that's truer to Lynch's philosophy and uses data we have. If you ever wire up an insider-data source (some paid Finnhub tiers, or FMP), the original check can be restored.
Inventory vs Sales Growth  computed
If inventory consistently grows faster than sales, the company is producing more than it's selling — an early sign of slowing demand. Lynch watched this as a recession indicator before the macro data caught up. Inverse signal: inventory shrinking while sales grow = lean operations.
Moat Rating (0–5)  screener-computed
Synthetic score from gross margin + ROIC + revenue growth consistency. Not a standard accounting metric — the screener's synthesis of what makes Buffett comfortable. Threshold: 3+ = meaningful competitive advantage exists.
Management Score (0–10)  screener-computed
Synthetic score from ROE + ROIC + FCF margin. Captures capital-allocation skill across multiple dimensions. The combination matters more than any single component — high ROE alone could be debt-fueled; ROIC alone could mask poor cash quality. Threshold: ≥7.
Cross-checking suggestion: When a number in the screener looks weird (extreme high, extreme low, or doesn't match your intuition), the fastest sanity-check is to open the ticker on StockAnalysis.com → Statistics tab. They show trailing, forward, and ex-NRI versions side-by-side so you can see exactly when the screener's number is being distorted by one-time accounting items.
💰 Fair Price & Entry Price

Two price columns help you answer the most important questions before buying: "Is this a bargain?" and "What price should I pay?" Both are derived directly from formulas in The Intelligent Investor by Benjamin Graham and Trading Price Action: Ranges by Al Brooks.

💰 Fair Price (Graham's Intelligent Investor, Chapter 11)
Primary: Graham's Growth Formula
Value = EPS × (7 + 1.5g) — conservative Graham
Where g = long-term earnings growth (10-yr EPS CAGR preferred, capped at 12%). This is a deliberately conservative tightening of Graham's original EPS × (8.5 + 2g) "foreshortened formula", which is widely considered too generous in modern markets — especially for fast growers, where the original can print fair values 50%+ above ours. Treat Fair as a rough margin-of-safety anchor, not a precise valuation.
Fallback: Graham Number
Value = √(22.5 × EPS × BVPS)
Used when growth data is missing. Graham's defensive investor rule of thumb: P/E × P/B should not exceed 22.5. So fair value lies at the price where that product equals exactly 22.5. Conservative, lower than the growth formula.
📈 Entry Price (Al Brooks, Trading Price Action: Ranges)

Brooks' core principle: the highest-quality entries are pullbacks to support, not breakouts. Buying at support means you can place a tight stop and have a known reward-to-risk ratio.

▲ UPTREND
Entry = EMA50. Wait for a deep pullback to the 50-day EMA — Brooks favors this over EMA20 because it gives better risk/reward and filters out shallow pullbacks that often fail.
◀▶ SIDEWAYS
Entry = support level. Range trade: buy near the bottom of the range, sell near the top. Use the recent swing low as the entry trigger.
↗ MILD TREND
Entry = EMA20. When the trend is real but not strongly directional, the shorter EMA acts as nearby support. More conservative than waiting for EMA50.
▼ DOWNTREND
Entry = "wait". Brooks rule: don't try to catch falling knives. In a confirmed downtrend, wait for trend reversal before considering an entry. No specific price target.
🎨 Color Coding in the Table
Fair column
🟢 Green = >5% below fair (undervalued — bargain)
🟡 Yellow = within ±5% of fair (fairly valued)
🔴 Red = >5% above fair (overvalued — pricey)
Entry column
🟢 Green = price at or below entry (buy now)
🟡 Yellow = needs <5% pullback to entry
⚪ "wait" = downtrend — don't catch knives
How to use both together:
  1. Fair Price tells WHAT to pay — the maximum reasonable price based on the business value
  2. Entry Price tells WHEN to pay — the technical setup that gives you the best risk/reward
  3. Best setup: Fair is green (undervalued) AND Entry is green (at/below technical support). This is rare — it means the market hates a stock that the math says is cheap and the chart says is at support.
  4. Common setup: Fair is green but Entry needs pullback. The stock is cheap but you can get it cheaper. Set a price alert at entry level.
  5. Avoid: Fair is red AND Entry says "wait." Overvalued in a downtrend. No reason to buy.
Honest limitations:
  • Graham's formula assumes growth will continue at the rate it has been — for declining businesses, it overestimates value
  • For unprofitable companies (negative EPS), the formula breaks down — we fall back to a book-value-based estimate
  • Entry Price assumes the trend continues — a strong trend reversal invalidates the setup
  • These are starting points for analysis, not buy signals. Always read the AI summary and check recent news.
🏦 Benjamin Graham — Safety & Value

Graham's approach: buy stocks trading significantly below intrinsic value with a margin of safety. He was Buffett's mentor and wrote The Intelligent Investor. His criteria filter for cheap, safe, financially sound companies.

Adequate Size ≥ $2B
Graham's first defensive criterion — exclude small caps "subject to more than average vicissitudes." ~$2B is Zweig's modern update of Graham's ≥$100M sales rule.
P/E < 15
Price-to-earnings below 15x. Above 25x is expensive. The market is paying less than 15 years of earnings — classic value territory.
P/E × P/B ≤ 22.5
Graham's combined value rule (Ch.14 #7): the product of P/E and P/B should stay under 22.5 (= 15 × 1.5). A low P/E can offset a higher P/B — more flexible than two rigid gates.
Current Ratio ≥ 2
Current assets are 2x current liabilities. Strong short-term financial health — won't need emergency financing anytime soon.
Debt/Equity ≤ 0.5
Conservative debt (a proxy for Graham's "long-term debt ≤ working capital"). The company can weather downturns without being crushed by interest payments.
Earnings Stability
No yearly deficit — Graham wanted positive earnings every year for a decade. We proxy with positive ROE across available history (or current profitability when history is missing).
EPS Growth > 3%
Sustained earnings growth (~33% over the decade in Graham's rule). Proves a durable business, not a one-hit wonder. Driven by the 5-yr EPS growth field.
Pays a Dividend
Any dividend signals financial maturity and management's confidence in cash flow. (Graham's defensive screen wants a 20-year record, which the free data tier can't source.)
Best used for: Long-term buy-and-hold of undervalued blue chips. Look for Graham 5+/8 combined with Buffett 4+/8. Pure Graham stocks are often boring — utilities, financials, consumer staples — that the market ignores.
🎩 Warren Buffett — Quality & Moat

Buffett evolved Graham's approach: buy wonderful companies at fair prices. He looks for durable competitive advantages (moats), exceptional management, and businesses that generate consistent free cash flow for decades.

Durable ROE
ROE ≥ 15% for 7+ years (or ≥18% currently when history is missing). Durability is the moat test — one merged criterion instead of double-counting current and historical ROE.
ROIC ≥ 12%
Return on invested capital. Measures how efficiently the business turns all capital (debt + equity) into profit. Above 12% = excellent.
Gross Margin ≥ 40%
High gross margins = pricing power. Companies with 40%+ gross margins can raise prices without losing customers — the hallmark of a moat.
Net Margin ≥ 15%
Profit after ALL costs. Pricing power that survives operating expenses and tax — the capital-light economics (See's, Coca-Cola) Buffett prizes. A distinct signal from gross margin.
FCF Margin ≥ 8%
Free cash flow as a share of revenue — real cash the business throws off. A genuine quality bar, unlike "FCF > 0" which almost any profitable company clears.
FCF / Net Income ≥ 0.8
Owner-earnings quality: cash actually backs the reported profit. Persistently low conversion is the classic accrual red flag — earnings not supported by cash.
Low Leverage (LT D/E < 50%)
Self-funding business — long-term debt under half of equity. Uses long-term D/E so it stays distinct from Graham's total-debt test rather than scoring the same thing twice.
Revenue Growth 5yr > 5%
Durable top-line growth — intrinsic value compounding over time. A wonderful business should be getting bigger, not just efficient.
Best used for: Core long-term holdings you'd own for 10+ years. Buffett 6+/8 stocks are rare — treasure them. Strong brands, network effects, switching costs, or cost advantages. A Buffett 7/8 or 8/8 deserves serious attention.
🔍 Peter Lynch — Growth at a Reasonable Price

Lynch managed Magellan Fund to 29% annual returns. His core method: first classify the company into one of six types (slow grower, stalwart, fast grower, cyclical, turnaround, asset play), then judge it by that type's yardstick — a slow grower is bought for its dividend, a fast grower for growth at a reasonable price. The screener detects the category and scores the growth/valuation/income criteria appropriate to it, rather than auto-rejecting everything that isn't a fast grower.

PEG ≤ 1 (dividend-adjusted)
Lynch's full rule: P/E divided by (growth rate + dividend yield). A fairly-priced grower has a P/E at or below its growth-plus-yield. Falls back to plain PEG when there's no dividend.
Balance Sheet (LT D/E < 30%)
Long-term debt under 30% of equity. Lynch "respected the balance sheet" — companies that don't need heavy debt to fund their story survive bad years.
Cash/Share > 0
Net cash per share — a cushion that reduces risk and funds growth without diluting shareholders.
Operating Leverage
Earnings growing at least as fast as sales (margin expansion) — the business gets more profitable as it scales, not just bigger.
Growth fits category
Judged against the category's yardstick: a fast grower wants 20–50% (the sweet spot; >50% is unsustainable), a stalwart 10–30%, a slow grower steady, a cyclical recovering sales.
Valuation fits category
Fair price for its type — a fast grower's P/E below its growth rate; a stalwart under 20; a slow grower under 15; a cyclical bought at a low (or negative) off-cycle P/E.
Income/safety fits category
A slow grower must pay a real dividend (>3%); a stalwart some income; a fast grower just needs to fund itself without heavy debt; a cyclical must survive the downturn.
Going concern
Not collapsing — earnings haven't cratered and revenue is growing over time. Replaces the old gate that auto-rejected every slow grower, turnaround and asset play.
Best used for: Finding growth stocks before Wall Street notices. Lynch 5+/8 with PEG under 1 is a strong signal. Often mid-cap companies in niche industries with strong earnings momentum and low analyst coverage.
📈 Al Brooks Technical Analysis

Al Brooks spent 30+ years developing a price action methodology built around a single 20-bar EMA (not a 20/50/200 stack), the "always-in" trend direction, and ranges defined by two-sided bar overlap rather than volatility. This is a long-only sleeve — it scores bullish setup quality, so a clean downtrend scores low. We compute 8 criteria from 250 days of real price data via Twelve Data.

Trend (always-in 20-EMA)
Most of the last 20 bars closed on one side of the 20-bar EMA (with an EMA-slope tiebreak) — Brooks's "always-in" direction. The single foundation of his method; no 50/200 stack.
Price above EMA20
Above the 20-bar EMA right now — a long-friendly position. The first line of support in a strong trend.
Trend Structure (HH/HL)
In an uptrend, each swing high and low is higher than the last — classic bullish structure. Scored bullish-only (a tidy downtrend earns nothing here).
Pullback to 20-EMA
In an uptrend, price has pulled back near the 20-EMA and the last bar is turning back up — Brooks's high-2/low-2 with-trend entry. Buy the dip, not the rip.
Breakout + follow-through
Near the 60-day high AND closing as a strong bull bar — a breakout with conviction, not a weak poke at the high that fails.
Strong Trend Bars
Most of the last five bars closed above the 20-EMA — sustained buying pressure behind the move.
Not Trading Range
Not sideways chop. Detected by two-sided bar overlap with a flat EMA (Brooks's actual definition) — not by volatility, which mislabels violent ranges and quiet trends.
No Climax/Reversal
The last bar isn't a parabolic blow-off (an exhaustion climax) or a strong reversal bar against the trend — Brooks's "don't buy the climax" caution.
How to use: Technical score 6+/8 = strong setup. 4–5 = proceed carefully. Below 4 = wait. Even perfect fundamentals (24/24) with Tech 1/8 means now is not the right time. Use SPY's technical score as a market regime filter.

🎯 Options Strategy — 6-Month Calls

The screener focuses on buying call options with ~6 month expiry. Not day trading — a directional bet that a fundamentally strong, technically bullish stock will be higher in 6 months.

⚠ Risk warning: Options can expire worthless — you can lose 100% of the premium paid. Never risk more than 1–5% of your portfolio on a single options position. Only use options on stocks you'd be happy owning as shares.
Conservative — ATM Strike
At-the-money call. Highest delta (~0.5), most expensive premium, highest probability of profit. Best for high-conviction setups.
Moderate — ~5% OTM Strike
Slightly out-of-the-money. Cheaper premium, higher leverage. Needs a bigger move to profit. Good balance of risk/reward.
Aggressive — ~10% OTM Strike
Deep out-of-the-money. Cheapest, highest leverage, lowest probability. Only for strong BUY setups with clear upcoming catalysts.
Trade Management Rules
📅 Expiry
Buy options with ~6 months to expiry. This gives the trade time to work and reduces daily theta (time decay) impact.
✅ Take profit
Exit at 50–100% gain. Don't get greedy — options give up gains fast. Up 50% in 2 months? Consider taking half off the table.
🛑 Stop loss
Exit if down 50%. A 50% loss means the thesis is wrong — cut it rather than hoping for recovery. Max loss = premium paid.
📆 Time stop
Exit 30 days before expiry regardless of P&L. Theta decay accelerates massively in the last 30 days — even if stock moves your way.
⚡ Earnings warning
The screener flags earnings within 6 weeks. IV spikes before earnings then collapses after (IV crush) — can destroy option value even if the stock moves your way.
📊 IV context
If IV is elevated, options are expensive. Consider waiting for IV to drop, or use a spread instead of a naked call to reduce premium cost.
Practical workflow: Total /32 ≥ 22 + Tech ≥ 6 + Options verdict = GO → open Options tab → check earnings warning → look up ATM strike bid/ask on your broker → size 1–3% of portfolio → set calendar reminder 30 days before expiry to exit.

🚀 Start Here — What this app does & how to use it

This is a value-investing stock screener for retail investors. It scores stocks across four classic frameworks (Graham, Buffett, Lynch, Brooks), gives you fair price + entry price targets grounded in those books, tracks your real portfolio positions, and uses AI to generate analysis on demand. The goal: bring disciplined, math-based stock research within reach without expensive Bloomberg-style tools.

🎯 What it answers for you
🔍 "Is this stock worth buying?"
A clear /32 score combining fundamentals, growth, and price action. Color-coded BUY/HOLD/SELL signal — see Scores & Signals tabs.
💰 "What price should I pay?"
Fair Price (Graham's formula) = max sensible price. Entry Price (Brooks) = technical setup price — see Fair & Entry tab.
📊 "How are my positions doing?"
Import Robinhood CSV → live P&L, auto alerts on time stops & earnings, AI guidance per position — see Portfolio tab.
🔭 "What new stocks should I look at?"
5 curated universes scan for breakouts, quality, value, options setups — see Discover tab.
📋 Your first 10 minutes
  1. Add API keys — click ⚙ API Keys in the top right. You need Anthropic (for AI), Finnhub (fundamentals), and Twelve Data (price history). All free tiers.
  2. Wait for default watchlist to load — 15 popular stocks start analyzing automatically (~2 min). Watch the green progress bar at top.
  3. Scan the table — green "BUY" badges + high scores = strongest candidates. Color-coded Fair/Entry columns tell you valuation + timing.
  4. Click any row to expand — see the full breakdown across Graham/Buffett/Lynch/Technical tabs.
  5. Click 🧠 Generate AI Summary on a stock that interests you — gets a thorough investment thesis with real web-searched catalysts (~10 sec).
  6. Add your real positions — Portfolio tab → 📂 Import CSV (Robinhood: Account → Statements → Export). See live P&L immediately.
  7. Try a Discover scan — Discover tab → click 🔍 Scan on any category (e.g. "Long-term Quality"). ~7 min to surface new candidates.
📖 The frameworks in plain language
🏦 Graham (Safety)
Is this stock cheap? Low P/E, low P/B, strong balance sheet, pays a dividend. Graham's defensive criteria filter out expensive and risky stocks.
🎩 Buffett (Quality)
Is this a great business? High ROE, strong free cash flow, fat margins, predictable returns. Buffett buys quality at fair prices, not garbage at cheap prices.
🔍 Lynch (Growth)
Is this growing? Reasonable price relative to growth (PEG < 1), expanding revenue and earnings. Lynch wants growth without overpaying.
📈 Brooks (Technicals)
Is the price action right? Trend direction, EMA positions, breakouts, pullbacks. Even a great business is a bad trade if you buy at the wrong time.
⏰ Suggested routine
📅 Daily (5 min)
1. Open Screener → glance at sorted scores. 2. Check Portfolio for new alerts (time stops, earnings warnings). 3. Act on anything urgent.
📆 Weekly (15 min)
1. Click ↻ Refresh all on Screener. 2. Sort by Total Score → spot anyone dropping from BUY to HOLD. 3. Run 🧠 AI Summary all to keep theses fresh. 4. Click 🧠 Review Portfolio to catch concentration risk early.
📊 Monthly (30 min)
1. Re-import Robinhood CSV. 2. Run one Discover scan (~7 min) — rotate categories. 3. Add interesting Discover results to watchlist. 4. Review per-position AI guidance, make trim/hold decisions deliberately.
🎯 Quarterly (1 hr)
1. Run all 5 Discover scans over a few days. 2. Compare portfolio to opportunities — anything to swap? 3. Tax loss harvesting check (Nov/Dec). 4. Rebalance if any one position is >20% of portfolio.
The 3 things this app helps you do well:
  1. Resist hype — Scores tell you when fundamentals AND technicals align. The chart can lie, the numbers can lie, but rarely at the same time.
  2. Catch problems early — Portfolio alerts surface issues before they become big losses (options near expiry, score deterioration).
  3. Find better opportunities — Discover scans surface quality stocks you'd never have found scrolling Twitter or Reddit.
